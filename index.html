A Beginner’s Introduction to Investing

All types of investments involve some level of risk, so it’s crucial to understand how your money might be impacted. Risks differ between investments, meaning the chances of profit or loss vary depending on the type of investment you choose.

This guide will help you with:

  • Grasping the basics

  • Clarifying your financial objectives

  • Exploring various investment options

  • Getting started and recognizing potential risks

Step 1: What are the fundamentals of investing?

At its core, investing is when you buy something with the expectation that it will grow in value over time. However, investments come with no guarantees, and you could receive back less than you initially invested.

When you start investing, you don’t need to commit a large amount of money right away. It’s important to only invest an amount you’re comfortable with.

There are several options for investing, including stocks, shares, and funds.

Step 2: Why do people choose to invest?

If you have savings and want to potentially grow them over the long term, investing might be an option to consider.

You can also save for the future in cash accounts, which offer interest that can provide additional income and access to your money if needed. The downside to cash savings is that inflation can erode their value over time.

Step 3: What’s your purpose for investing?

Investing for income

If your goal is to generate income from your investments, you could focus on those that offer regular payouts. For instance, some stocks provide dividends, while bonds pay interest.

Investing for growth

Investing for growth aims to increase the value of your investments, also known as capital appreciation. For example, if you invest in stocks, growth occurs when the stock price increases.

Compound growth

Reinvesting the income from your investments can speed up growth and help you achieve your financial objectives more quickly. Both your original investment and the reinvested income will benefit from any additional growth.

Most investors aim for a balance of both growth and income. For example, an income-focused investor might reinvest the income they receive, while a growth-focused investor might sell their investments to generate income.

Step 4: Your Investment options

Shares

When you buy shares, you're purchasing ownership in a company. Shares are traded on the stock exchange throughout the day, and their prices can fluctuate.

  • Pros: Potential for Capital Gains If you carefully select your stocks, their value could increase over time. Historically, shares have offered better long-term returns than cash investments, though this is not guaranteed.
  • Cons: Risk of Capital Losses If you invest in a company that doesn’t grow in value, the share price may decline, which could result in a loss of your invested capital.

Funds

A fund is a type of collective investment, meaning your money is pooled with that of other investors to purchase a variety of assets. These assets might include shares, bonds, cash, and more, helping to diversify your risk.

  • Pros: Diversification Funds invest in a wide range of sectors, markets, and stocks, which can spread out the risk. If one investment performs poorly, others may perform better, reducing potential losses to your overall portfolio.
  • Cons: Liquidity Risks Some funds, like property funds, may need to sell assets to return money to investors who want to withdraw. If the assets are hard to sell, you may face delays in receiving your money.

Exchange Traded Funds (ETFs)

Exchange Traded Funds trade on a stock exchange like shares. However unlike shares, which are focused on one company, ETFs invest in a range of assets and are designed to track an index, a commodity, a sector or currency.

  • Pros: Lower fees The benefit of ETFs is cost effectiveness. They offer lower fees due to lower operating costs.
  • Cons: Performance Performance - ETFS are trackers - they aim to match market movements rather than trying to outperform it.
  • Please note: We are not able to trade or hold US listed ETFs.

Investment Trusts

An investment trust is a company that raises money through selling shares to investors and then pool the money to purchase and sell a range of investments. Investment trusts can vary with different aims and mixes of shares and assets

  • Pros: Potential returns Investment trusts have extra features such as being able to borrow money that may increase potential gains but also increase risk.
  • Cons: Potential Price Volatility The price of an investment trust can be influenced by the demand for the share. In a scenario where investors don’t feel the investment trusts is being managed as well as expected then this can impact the price when investors want to sell rather than buy.

Bonds and Gilts

Bonds and gilts are a way for companies or governments to raise money which is done by borrowing money from investors. When you invest in a bond or gilt you’re lending money to a company or government which in return provides a fixed rate of interest.

  • Pros: Stability Bonds and gilts have lower risks than stocks and have the potential to provide a more stable return over time.
  • Cons: Growth Potential The drawback of bonds and gilts is that they don’t provide higher long term returns compared to other stocks. Bonds and gilts can be impacted negatively by changes to interest rates, economic uncertainty and currency fluctuations.

Step 5: Are you ready to invest?

Before proceeding with your investment, please carefully review the following statements to ensure you agree with each of them:

  • You have at least three to six months of net income readily accessible to cover emergencies and any planned expenses in the near future.
  • You understand and accept that investing involves some level of risk.
  • You are prepared to invest for a minimum of five years.
  • You do not have significant short-term debts, as the interest on these debts may outweigh the returns from your investment.
  • You have conducted your own research and feel confident that now is the right time to invest.
